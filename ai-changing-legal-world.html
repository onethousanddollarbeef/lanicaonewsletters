How AI is Changing the Legal World

Editorial | 2026

Editorial Department of North American Legal Monthly

In the early 2020s, artificial intelligence transitioned from a niche experimental tool into a daily use within the public consciousness. This was mainly driven by consumer-facing technologies like OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Amazon’s Alexa, Google’s Waymo, etc. As it has steadily expanded across sectors like medicine, research, and general technology, its adoption within the legal profession became inevitable.

Today, the technology is actively transforming standard legal workflows. Legal research platforms can now leverage natural language processing to extract insights and generate concise summaries of complex case law. Similarly, advanced contract review software can utilize machine learning algorithms to scan massive amounts of documents and forms, while also flagging high-risk clauses. By automating these data-heavy, time-consuming tasks, AI is fundamentally altering how legal professionals manage research, analyze risks, and serve their clients.

As artificial intelligence moves into the legal mainstream, platforms like CoCounsel act as digital assistants for professionals. It streamlines practice management through three core capabilities:

Legal and Tax Research: Connecting directly to authoritative industry databases to gather facts and analyze complex legal data.

Document and Contract Analysis: Extracting critical insights and identifying risks across massive volumes of compliance documents and contracts.

Workflow Integration: Embedding seamlessly into daily applications like Microsoft 365 and existing document management systems to optimize reach and efficiency.

The success of these features has triggered a wave of AI tools across the industry, with law firms quickly adopting them to handle data-heavy workloads. However, this rapid technological shift has also caught the attention of regulators, prompting the recent emergence of new court policies designed to govern AI use in the legal world.

One policy was instituted by the New York State Unified Court System (NYS UCS). NYS UCS’s Part 161: Use of Artificial Intelligence Technology applies to all courts of the Unified Court System. This policy establishes clear guidelines for all courts within the state’s unified system regarding how automated technologies can intersect with official legal processes. The term “artificial intelligence” or “AI” is defined as a machine-based system that can give advice, decisions, or predictions in response to human-defined objectives. It can put such perceptions into models through analysis in an automated manner and use model inference for information or action. “Paper” is defined as a brief, memo, affidavit, or other document prepared by an attorney for submission to court. Notably, this does not cover rules for evidentiary materials, which are subject to other requirements. The policy states that AI tools may be used in preparing papers for a court, and attorneys and parties are not required to disclose to the court that they have used AI. However, filers must carefully review the document, because once the document is submitted, the filer has ownership over all facts stated in the document and certifies that there is no fictitious content. By leaving accountability to the filer, New York aims to embrace AI efficiency while heavily guarding against the risk of AI “hallucinations.”

The NYS UCS Part 161 policy sets a clear precedent for how the legal world will coexist with advanced technology. By eliminating the requirement for explicit AI disclosure, the court frames generative AI not as an independent entity, but as an advanced utility, similar to a spellchecker or research assistant, that still requires human verification. While this rule specifically targets formal court “papers,” it signals an inevitable expansion of AI into broader legal operations.

As AI is integrated deeper into law firms, data privacy quickly surfaces as a central concern. The pressing challenge for firms is ensuring that confidential client information does not inadvertently become fuel for an AI model’s continuous learning cycle. Without stringent data governance and enterprise-grade safeguards, proprietary information fed into these platforms risks being stored, leaked, or used to train tools that could benefit competitors and violate privacy laws.

To address pressing privacy concerns, the legal tech industry has established rigorous security standards to safeguard sensitive information. Many enterprise-grade legal AI vendors now enforce strict zero-data retention policies, ensuring that client data is never permanently stored, leaked, or used to train public algorithms. When choosing between platforms, firms are encouraged to look for specific institutional safeguards and compliance metrics:

Security Certifications: Leading platforms demonstrate SOC 2 and ISO 27001 compliance, which are international standards showing that security, availability, and confidentiality controls are continuously audited.

Technical Infrastructure: Safe workflows should use advanced encryption alongside strict access controls to restrict data visibility.

Human Oversight: In addition to backend security, users should actively audit AI outputs to check for bias, inaccuracies, or unusual patterns.

Ultimately, legal practitioners can mitigate risk by ensuring they upload client data only to AI platforms that explicitly guarantee no information will be repurposed for secondary functions or external training.

For clients, these emerging guidelines should offer peace of mind rather than concern about the growing AI landscape. The legal industry embracing AI does not mean client data is being cast into the world. Rather, it signifies that firms are equipped with more powerful tools to advocate for their clients’ interests efficiently. When a firm pairs human empathy and legal expertise with enterprise-grade, audited AI platforms, clients benefit from faster turnaround times and deeper insights without sacrificing the sanctity of attorney-client privilege. Ultimately, these technological boundaries ensure that while the tools of the trade are evolving, the absolute commitment to client confidentiality and client interests remains uncompromised.
