USCIS Launches New System for Background Checks; Large Number of Cases Blocked

On April 28, according to information disclosed internally by the USCIS Verification Division, all family-based immigration applicants may be asked two core questions during their interviews: whether they have ever suffered harm or harassment in their home country, and whether they are afraid to return. These two questions directly correspond to the key legal elements of asylum. If an applicant answers "yes" or refuses to answer related questions, the immigration officer may suspend their green card application. This new regulation applies to almost all non-immigrant visa categories, including B visas (business/tourism), F visas (students), J visas (exchange visitors), as well as H, L, O, and P employment visas, and covers all derivatives (dependent applicants). It aims to completely screen out applicants who enter the U.S. on non-immigrant visas and later apply for asylum, enforcing stricter verification of visa purpose authenticity from the source.

Cross-State Marriage Fraud Green Card Ring Busted, Many Sentenced to Prison

ICE recently smashed a cross-state "marriage fraud" green card ring. According to reports, the syndicate arranged fraudulent marriages between U.S. citizens and foreign nationals to obtain permanent residency status. Currently, multiple suspects have pleaded guilty, including several military personnel. The investigation showed that the ring operated in multiple states over a long period, involving Florida, New York, Connecticut, and Nevada. Through careful planning, they staged fake photos, fabricated cohabitation records, and coached "real marriages" to pass interviews. They also used a installment payment system: a deposit upon registration, a payment after the interview, and the final installment after divorce. Under U.S. law, once deemed a fraudulent marriage, individuals face criminal charges, permanent deportation, or the loss of eligibility to apply for immigration status in the future.

Former U.S. Mayor Stripped of U.S. Citizenship: Identity Reversal Officially Begins?

The Department of Justice filed a civil lawsuit against Philippe Bien-Aime, the former mayor of North Miami, Florida, alleging that he committed material misrepresentations and fraud during his naturalization process, and requested the revocation of his U.S. citizenship. According to disclosures from the Department of Justice, Bien-Aime originally used a fraudulent passport to enter the U.S. and was ordered deported in 2001. He subsequently obtained legal status, a green card, and naturalization through a series of procedures, including marriage, without reporting his prior deportation history, marriage status, or personal information. Federal authorities successfully identified him through fingerprint comparison, confirming that the individual registered as "Janvier" in his early records is the same person as "Bien-Aime." The case is currently in litigation, and the relevant charges await a judicial ruling.

H-1B Suspended for Three Years? Heavyweight Congressional Proposal Stuns Immigrants in the U.S.

Arizona Republican Representative Eli Crane officially introduced the "H-1B Visa Suspension Act of 2026," which proposes a systemic overhaul of the H-1B visa system. The core content includes: proposing a complete suspension of new H-1B visa issuances for the next three years; reducing the annual quota from 65,000 to 25,000 in the future and eliminating some quota-exempt categories; raising high-wage thresholds (tentatively setting the minimum annual salary at $200,000) to strengthen the "high-end talent guidance." In addition, the proposal adjusts multi-item structures, including prohibiting multiple employers or concurrent jobs, banning outsourcing enterprises, restricting remote work, banning green card extensions, and eliminating OPT. It is worth noting that the proposal is currently only in the initial legislative stage and will not affect the H-1B application market in the short term. However, the signal it sends reveals a trend that the future immigration system will become "higher threshold, stricter supervision, and more American-worker-prioritized."

Birthright Citizenship to Change? Supreme Court Opens Hearing

On April 1st, the Supreme Court held oral arguments for the birthright citizenship case Trump v. Barbara. During the hearing, several justices raised sharp questions regarding executive power and constitutional interpretations, indicating immense debate at the legal level. Analysts pointed out that the core of the case lies in how to interpret the Fourteenth Amendment's "birthright citizenship" principle, and whether the executive branch has the authority to adjust it through executive orders. Currently, the case is still under deliberation, and a final ruling has not been made. According to convention, the Supreme Court usually releases major case rulings before the end of June. The decision is expected as early as late May or potentially in June. If the executive order is ultimately sustained, it will not only change the citizenship rules for some newborns but could also trigger widespread executive actions, including background checks on the status and residency of the newborns' parents, thereby increasing overall administrative enforcement. This case is considered the most impactful immigration law dispute in recent years, and its outcome will have a profound effect on the Chinese immigrant community.

White House State Dinner Shooting: Suspect Charged with "Attempted Assassination of the President"

On April 25th, a shooting incident occurred at a White House state dinner. Law enforcement authorities reported that a suspect broke through the security checkpoint and opened fire. Upon discovering the situation, Secret Service agents quickly swarmed and neutralized the suspect. The president was safely evacuated and unharmed. According to federal indictments, the suspect has been officially charged, facing multiple severe federal charges including "conspiracy to assassinate the president." Initial investigations show the suspect carried a shotgun and a handgun, attempting to approach the core area of the event. Although security personnel suffered injuries due to defensive gear, no serious consequences were caused. After the incident, relevant agencies launched a comprehensive review of the security gaps at the venue, and federal authorities are conducting a deep investigation into whether co-conspirators exist. The case is in the early stages of judicial proceedings, and the suspect has not been sentenced; further details are under investigation.

Highest Court Rules Trump Tariffs Invalid! $160 Billion Must or Needs to be Refunded

The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that the tariff increases imposed by the Trump administration under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) exceeded statutory authorization. The justices pointed out that while IEEPA grants the president broad authority to regulate economic activities during national emergencies, it does not clearly authorize the imposition of tariffs to increase financial revenue. The ruling states that under the Constitution, the power to levy taxes belongs to Congress, and the executive branch cannot act without explicit statutory authorization. The ruling will directly lead to the invalidation of multiple tariff measures, including the "unwinding of customs duties" and partial refunds for targeted countries, involving approximately $160 billion in tariff refund processing.
